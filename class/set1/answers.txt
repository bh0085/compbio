The above list indicates a marked tendency for very strongly conserved sequences (e.g: elements 0-10 in the above list) to correspond to known motifs with high specificity (i.e: few non-specific codons such as N,W,R...). That this should hold true is clear: our ordering has favored bases whose specific identity are exactly conserved (indicated by * in the alignment).

Looking at the above list (list c), an apparent trend of decreasing fractional conservation implying proportionally decreased motif identification seems clear. Choosing to match hexamers with motifs for simply the most frequently occuring hexamers (list d, below) reveals a similarly high fraction of motif identification per hexamer including functionality related to:

Cell Cycle: MCM1A
Meiosis: XBP1
Stress Response: ESR2, RLM1(*) (Cell wall integrity), HSF1 (Chaperones)
Other, essential: ARS(*)
Transcription: TBP (TATA Box binding for rpoII)

On the basis of hexamer identification alone, both sequence classes (highly numerous and highly conserved) appear to encode a range of biological functions. Growth-essential functionality (e.g, meiosis and mitosis) appears more frequently in highly conservered motifs but (again, on the basis of hexamer identication alone), they are not completely restricted: indeed, TATA box binding protein appears in highly non-conserved regions.(**)

(*): At the hexamer level it is hard to unambiguously indicate for e.g, ARS and RLM motifs.
