b) Change score to distance metric:
The standard prescription to go from an affinity to a distance is to let D(A,B)^2 = Affinity(A,A) + Affinity(B,B) - 2*Affinity(A,B). 

In this case, affinity should be the scored alignment of a sequence with another (where Aff(A,A) is simply |A| times the quantity along the diagonal of the scoring matrix). Since we are looking for a distance measure that is linear in base changes, we need not take a square root of the quantity on the RHS. That such a distance is nonnegative is clear:

(Aff(A,A) + Aff(B,B) > 2* Aff(A,B)) 

and to see that it is monotone decreasing as alignment score increases is also clear:

(Decrease the quality of the alignment and Aff(A,B) falls while Aff(A,A) and Aff(B,B) are fixed for fixed |A|,|B|)

We can see that the triangle inequality holds: D(A,B) + D(B,C) >= D(A,C) by noting that aligning A->B->C can be expressed as a suboptimal traversal of a scoring matrix similar to the one used in the actual alignment of A->C. Moreover, in so far as distance counts the number of changes required to go from A->B, it is trivially clear that the triangle inequality holds - the number of changes to go from A-B-C is greater than (or equal to) the number from A-C alone.

4bi: pie chart a) (BLUE)  showing the fraction of hexamers significant to pvalue = (.05 / 4096), those passing the Bonfferri correction	    
     	       b) (GREEN) showing the number significant to pvalue = .05 but not passing Bonferri.

4bii:A scatterplot of hexamer instances showing:
       a) Instances colored from black to red denoting high - low AT content (AT content in [0,6]). 
       b) On the x axis the total number of occurence for a hexamer.
       c) On the y axis, the pvalue of a hexamer.
       d) Translucent blue circles indicating that a hexamer is one of the hundred most conserved by fraction.
       e) A few labeled hexamers.
       
We can see that though highly conserved instances tend to have a low pvalue, they are not uniformly distributed in the space of AT values - indeed, they are clustered towards high GC content and low occurence number. High pvalue instances on the other hand, sample the space of AT values more evenly. On one hand, the pvalue is a more statistically reliable indicator of significance but on the other, pvalue may be tricked somewhat by the different topologies of AT and GC rich sequences in the genome. In particular, ATs are likely to occur in repeats and be multiply counted as hexamer instances. Indeed, as fig4bii indicates, both ATATAT and TTTTTT are found to occur highly frequently with somewhat low significance values.
